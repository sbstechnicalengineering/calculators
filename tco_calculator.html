Professional Supply Chain Guide to Total Cost of Ownership

Why the unit price on a PO is one of the least important numbers in your procurement decision, and what world-class supply chain professionals measure instead.

The Procurement Iceberg: What Your PO Price Does Not Show

Why price-only thinking is a supply chain liability

In most organizations, the buyer's primary performance metric is Purchase Price Variance (PPV) - the difference between what they paid and a standard cost. A lower unit price generates a favorable PPV. The buyer looks good. The CFO sees a win. But this creates a powerful incentive to optimize for a single visible number while ignoring a much larger set of costs absorbed by operations, finance, quality, and the shop floor.

The CAPS Research finding: Purchase price represents only 25 to 40% of the total cost of a purchased component when all supply chain, quality, inventory, and transactional costs are properly allocated.1 For C-class parts like fasteners, the hidden costs can exceed the purchase price itself.

How PPV Purchasing Moves Costs to Other Departments

The hidden tax of winning on price

Purchase Price Variance (PPV) is one of the most widely used and most dangerous metrics in procurement. When buyers are rewarded for PPV, they are rationally incentivized to buy at the lowest unit price, regardless of total organizational cost.

The organizational cost transfer: A fastener buyer secures a 40% unit price reduction from a Shenzhen supplier. Their PPV looks excellent. Meanwhile: the warehouse absorbs 3 months of MOQ overstock. Finance carries the working capital. Quality sorts two defective lots. Production expedites after stockouts. The savings the buyer captured never materialized - they were distributed to departments with no visibility into the sourcing decision.

Why SBS can be lower on total cost: SBS is built around the sourcing, procurement, logistics, planning, warehousing, and point-of-use delivery of hardware. A manufacturer's core competence is usually designing, assembling, and selling its own product, not managing thousands of low-dollar C-class transactions. The Quality Cost Trap: One Bad Lot Wipes Out the Year's Savings

Why quality risk is almost never priced into sourcing decisions

The Cost of Quality (COQ) framework (Crosby, Juran) divides quality costs into prevention, appraisal, internal failure, and external failure.9 When a buyer selects an offshore supplier based on unit price, they implicitly accept higher failure costs - which almost always live outside the procurement budget.

The math procurement meetings rarely do: A fastener costs $0.45 from offshore vs. $0.63 from SBS. On 100,000 units, that is $18,000 in apparent savings. One quality event: 12 hrs sort labor at $35/hr plus net scrap cost at 70% customer absorption = ~$1,732. Two events plus carrying cost, freight, tariffs, and transaction costs: the cheaper supplier costs more before year end.
The SBS Quality Commitment: When SBS supplies a part and a quality issue arises, SBS will sort the affected lot, replace nonconforming parts, or arrange rework at no charge. This transfers quality risk entirely to SBS. No supplier dispute, no partial credit negotiation, no sort labor cost to the customer.

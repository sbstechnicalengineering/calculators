Process Capability — Theory & Practice

A reference guide to understanding Cp, Cpk, Pp, Ppk, PPM, and the statistical foundations of process capability studies in manufacturing and quality engineering.

1What Is Process Capability?

Process capability is a statistical measure of a manufacturing process's ability to produce output that consistently falls within specified engineering tolerances. It answers the question: "Is our process capable of meeting customer requirements?"

All processes exhibit natural variation. The goal of capability analysis is to quantify that variation relative to the tolerance window defined by the Lower Specification Limit (LSL) and Upper Specification Limit (USL). The wider the specification window compared to the process spread, the more capable the process.

Process capability is expressed as a ratio of two quantities:

  • Available room — defined by the specification limits (USL − LSL)
  • Required room — defined by the natural spread of the process (6σ or 6s)
Key Principle: A capable process does not just produce parts within spec today — it reliably produces parts within spec over time, even under normal process variation.

2Specification Limits: LSL & USL

LSL (Lower Specification Limit) is the minimum acceptable value for a given feature. Any measurement below this value is considered non-conforming.

USL (Upper Specification Limit) is the maximum acceptable value. Any measurement above it is non-conforming.

Specification limits are set by engineering design requirements, customer agreements, or regulatory standards. They are not the same as control limits, which are statistical boundaries derived from the process data itself.

Common Mistake: Setting specification limits based on what the process currently produces, rather than what the design actually requires. This inflates apparent capability and masks real engineering risk.

When only one specification limit exists (e.g., flatness must be ≤ 0.005 mm — no lower limit), only Cpu/Ppu or Cpl/Ppl is meaningful, not Cp/Pp.

3Cp and Pp — Potential Capability (No Centering)

Both Cp and Pp measure the ratio of the specification width to the process spread. Neither accounts for whether the process is centered within the specification.

Cp =(USL − LSL) / 6σσ from control chart moving range
Pp =(USL − LSL) / 6ss = calculated std deviation from all data

The critical difference:

  • Cp uses σ — estimated from the average moving range (R̄/d₂). This captures within-subgroup short-term variation only, assuming the process is in statistical control.
  • Pp uses s — the standard deviation calculated from all data points. This captures total historical variation, including any shifts, trends, or instability over time.
Interpretation: If Cp ≈ Pp, the process is in statistical control — the two variation estimates agree. If Cp ≫ Pp, special-cause variation is inflating the overall standard deviation, signaling an unstable process.

4Cpk and Ppk — Centering-Adjusted Capability

Cp and Pp assume the process is perfectly centered between the specification limits. In practice, the process mean (x̄) is rarely exactly at the midpoint of the tolerance. Cpk and Ppk correct for this by measuring distance from the mean to the nearest specification limit.

Cpu =(USL − x̄) / 3σ
Cpl =(x̄ − LSL) / 3σ
Cpk =min(Cpu, Cpl)worst-case direction determines Cpk
Ppk =min(Ppu, Ppl)same logic using s instead of σ

Because Cpk uses the nearest limit, it will always be ≤ Cp. The difference between Cp and Cpk reflects how off-center the process mean is:

  • Cp = Cpk → the process is perfectly centered
  • Cp > Cpk → the process mean is shifted toward one specification limit
  • Cpk < 0 → the mean is outside a specification limit entirely

7Short-Term vs. Long-Term Capability

You may encounter the terms "short-term" and "long-term" capability used to describe Cpk and Ppk respectively. While this is common industry shorthand, it can be misleading:

  • Cp / Cpk (using σ from moving range) reflects variation within subgroups — effectively the best the process can do when it is operating consistently. Some call this "short-term" or "potential" capability.
  • Pp / Ppk (using s from all data) reflects all variation over the entire study period — including shifts between subgroups, time-based drift, and special causes. Some call this "long-term" or "actual" capability.
Dr. Donald Wheeler's view: There is no short-term and long-term capability — only capability estimated from within-subgroup variation versus capability estimated from overall variation. If the process is in statistical control, both estimates will agree.

When Cpk ≫ Ppk, it means the process has experienced instability or shifts over time that the within-subgroup σ cannot detect. This is a signal to investigate assignable causes and improve process stability — not simply to report Ppk and move on.

9PPM — Parts Per Million

PPM (Parts Per Million) is a defect rate metric expressing how many parts out of one million would be expected to be non-conforming, assuming a normal distribution. It is derived directly from the probability estimates:

PPM below= Pr(x < LSL) × 1,000,000
PPM above= Pr(x > USL) × 1,000,000
PPM total= (PPM below) + (PPM above)

PPM is intuitive for communicating defect rates to non-technical stakeholders. A process with Cpk = 1.33 produces approximately 63 defective parts per million. A Six Sigma process produces fewer than 3.4 PPM (accounting for a 1.5σ shift).

Note: PPM calculated from the normal distribution is a theoretical estimate. Actual defect rates may differ if the process is not normally distributed or if the distribution has heavy tails. Always validate the normality assumption for critical features.

10Sample Size & Best Practices

The accuracy of capability indices depends heavily on sample size. The following guidelines apply:

  • Minimum 30 samples recommended for initial capability studies (AIAG MSA/SPC guidelines)
  • 50–125 samples for PPAP submission studies (typically 25 subgroups of 5)
  • Samples should be representative of normal production conditions — not cherry-picked
  • Measurements should be taken in time order to detect trends and assess control
  • Validate the measurement system (Gauge R&R) before collecting data — gauge variation must be small relative to the tolerance
  • Check for normality (Shapiro-Wilk, Anderson-Darling) before interpreting capability indices
Gauge R&R Rule: Measurement system variation should be less than 10% of the tolerance (or 10% of the process variation). If gauge R&R exceeds 30%, the measurement system is unacceptable and capability indices are unreliable.

Core Formula
ROP = Adjusted Daily Demand × Effective Lead Time + Safety Stock
Effective LT = (LT Breakdown + OTD Buffer)  |  OTD Buffer = LT × (1 − OTD%)

Safety Stock Formulas
Manual: SS = Manual Qty × Volatility Factor
Days of Supply: SS = Adj. Daily Demand × Safety Days × Volatility Factor
Statistical: SS = Z × √(LT × σd² + d̄² × σLT²)  (Volatility factor not applied; σd auto-set from CV% if entered)

CV% → σd: σd = Daily Demand × (CV% / 100)

Inventory Planning Best Practices & Theory

Educational reference summarizing commonly accepted inventory planning concepts, reorder point theory, and practical supply chain guidance.

Core Inventory Planning Concepts
Reorder Point (ROP)
A reorder point too low increases stockout risk. A reorder point too high increases working capital and carrying costs.

General Formula: ROP = Average Demand During Lead Time + Safety Stock

Expanded Formula: ROP = (Daily Demand × Seasonality) × Total Lead Time + Safety Stock
where Total Lead Time = Production LT + Transit + Inspection + Buffer
Safety Stock Method Selection Guide
Manual Override — Use when you have a known target quantity, contractual minimum, or prior agreement. Apply Low volatility to pass through your value unchanged. Volatility factor scales this upward for higher risk.

Days of Supply — Use when thinking in days of coverage is intuitive. Simple and transparent. Safety Stock = Adjusted Daily Demand × Safety Days × Volatility Factor.

Statistical (Service Level) — Use when you have historical standard deviation data for both demand and lead time. The Z-score encodes the probability of stockout. Volatility factor is not additionally applied — the formula already incorporates variability mathematically.
When the Volatility Factor Applies
  • Manual and Days-of-Supply methods: The Volatility Factor multiplies safety stock upward. Low (×1.00) passes the value through unchanged. Critical (×1.50) increases it by 50%.
  • Statistical method: Volatility Factor is not applied. Risk is already encoded through the Z-score (service level) and the standard deviation inputs. Double-applying a volatility multiplier would overstate safety stock.
  • This is why the Volatility field is greyed out in Service Level mode — it shows you exactly what is and is not driving the output.
Lead Time Best Practices
  • Separate lead time into production, transit, receiving, and internal processing components.
  • Use actual historic lead time data whenever possible rather than supplier-quoted values only.
  • Track lead time variability, not only average lead time.
  • Long international supply chains generally require higher safety stock and more frequent review.
  • Quality containment, customs delays, and port congestion should be factored in when applicable.
Safety Stock Best Practices
  • Safety stock should increase when demand volatility or lead time variability increases.
  • Critical production parts typically justify higher service levels and more safety stock.
  • Low-cost / high-impact parts often justify additional protection relative to their carrying cost.
  • Use service-level calculations when historic demand and lead time variance data are available.
  • Review safety stock after stockouts, forecast misses, supplier quality events, or engineering changes.
  • Safety stock should not permanently compensate for chronic supplier performance problems.
Volatility / Risk Classification Guidance
Low Risk (×1.00) — Stable demand history, consistent supplier performance, strong forecast confidence, no significant stockout history.

Medium Risk (×1.15) — Moderate demand swings, occasional supplier or logistics variability, forecast uncertainty.

High Risk (×1.30) — Significant demand variability, supplier quality concerns, repeated shortages, launch timing uncertainty, or weak forecast confidence.

Critical Risk (×1.50) — Severe supply instability, constrained capacity, recurring stockouts, launch builds, or known business continuity concerns.
Common Drivers of Demand Volatility
  • Seasonality or launch builds
  • Engineering changes or platform transitions
  • Customer forecast instability
  • Quality containment or scrap events
  • Supply chain shortages
  • Production schedule changes
  • End-of-quarter shipment spikes
Inventory Turn Considerations
  • Higher inventory turns generally reduce working capital requirements.
  • Very high turns with low safety stock can increase stockout risk.
  • Low turns may indicate excessive inventory, obsolete inventory, MOQ constraints, or inaccurate forecasts.
  • Inventory targets should balance service level, operational continuity, and financial objectives.

Thread engineering best practices from decades of fastener application experience. These guidelines help engineers and buyers select the correct thread specification, understand fit implications, and prevent common assembly failures.

Plating and Coating Considerations

Pitch Diameter and Plating Thickness

Electroplating deposits material on all exposed surfaces including thread flanks. The effect on pitch diameter is significant and often underestimated.

Rule of thumb: Pitch diameter grows by approximately 4 times the plating thickness. A 0.0003 in zinc layer can add up to 0.0012 in to pitch diameter on a bolt.
  • Zinc electroplate: typically 0.0002 to 0.0005 in per side
  • Hot-dip galvanizing: 0.001 to 0.004 in per side
  • Cadmium plate: 0.0002 to 0.0004 in per side
  • Class 2A includes a built-in plating allowance in its lower limit

Hot-Dip Galvanizing (HDG)

HDG deposits substantially more zinc than electroplating. Standard 2A/2B fits will not assemble after galvanizing without modification.

  • Bolts: use Class 1A or specify oversize before galvanizing
  • Nuts: ASTM A563 requires oversize tapping for HDG nuts
  • Metric: use Class 8g for HDG external threads
  • Verify effective thread length after coating
Reference: ASTM F2329 (HDG bolts), ASTM A563 (HDG nuts). Oversize tapping per ASME B18.2.2.

Lubrication and Friction Coefficient

Plating type significantly affects friction coefficient (nut factor K) and therefore the torque-to-clamp-load relationship.

  • Zinc plate, dry: K approx 0.20
  • Cadmium plate: K approx 0.15 (very slippery)
  • Hot-dip galvanized dry: K approx 0.25 or higher
  • Wax or lubricant on plated: K approx 0.13 to 0.17
Tip: Confirm the nut factor K for your specific coating and lubrication before specifying torque values.
Thread Series Selection

Fine vs. Coarse Thread

Fine threads offer higher tensile stress area and better vibration resistance, but carry important trade-offs.

  • Fine thread: higher At, finer torque control, better vibration resistance
  • Fine thread: significantly more susceptible to cross threading
  • Fine thread: harder to assemble in dirty or corroded conditions
  • Coarse thread: more forgiving of damage, contamination, and misalignment
Recommendation: Use coarse threads for general structural applications. Reserve fine threads for precision assemblies with controlled alignment.

Thread Engagement Length

Insufficient thread engagement is a leading cause of joint failure. Minimum engagement depends on the relative strength of the fastener and tapped material.

  • Steel into steel: 1.0 x D minimum, 1.5 x D preferred
  • Steel into aluminum: 1.5 to 2.0 x D due to lower shear strength
  • Steel into cast iron: 1.5 x D minimum
  • Thread inserts restore full steel thread strength in soft materials
Tip: Tensile stress area (shown in results) is the bolt's weakest section. Engagement must be sufficient to strip the nut before the bolt breaks.

Cross Threading Prevention

Cross threading causes permanent damage and is far more common with fine threads due to smaller thread height.

  • Start fasteners by hand before applying tools
  • Thread should feel smooth with no resistance for first 2 to 3 turns
  • Resistance at start of engagement signals misalignment
  • Use chamfered or piloted lead-in features where possible
Rule: If it does not thread freely by hand, stop and realign. Never force a fastener that feels like it is binding.
Thread Class and Fit

Unified Thread Class Guide

ASME B1.1 defines three classes for both external (A) and internal (B) threads.

  • Class 1A/1B: Loose tolerance. Quick assembly in contaminated environments. Uncommon in structural use.
  • Class 2A/2B: General purpose. Most widely used for commercial and industrial fasteners. Includes plating allowance on external threads.
  • Class 3A/3B: Precision close fit. Aerospace, tooling, minimal play applications. No plating allowance built in.

Metric Thread Class Guide

ISO 965 uses a tolerance grade (number) and position (letter). Lowercase = external, uppercase = internal.

  • 6g/6H: General purpose. Equivalent to inch Class 2A/2B. Most common globally.
  • 4g6g: Precision external. Tighter pitch diameter tolerance. Aerospace and precision use.
  • 5H/4H: Precision internal. Closer to nominal than 6H.
  • 8g: Coarser tolerance for hot-dip galvanized external threads.
Convention: Lowercase letters (g, e, f) are external. Uppercase (G, H) are internal.

Tensile Stress Area

The tensile stress area (At) is the effective cross-section used to calculate thread load capacity.

  • At is always smaller than the nominal shank area
  • Fine threads have slightly higher At than coarse of the same diameter
  • Proof load, yield load, and ultimate load are all based on At
  • Inch: At = 0.7854 x (D - 0.9743/n) squared
  • Metric: At = 0.7854 x (D - 0.9382 x P) squared
Design check: Required bolt load must be less than At x Sp (proof strength) of the selected grade.

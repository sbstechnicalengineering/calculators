Fundamentals
Thread forming vs thread cutting
Thread forming displaces substrate material to create mating threads — no material is removed, no chips generated. Thread cutting uses a fluted or slotted point to remove material, producing chips as the thread is tapped into the substrate.
Chip generation impact
Chips from cutting screws must have somewhere to go. In blind holes or precision assemblies, trapped chips cause jamming, contamination, or false torque readings. Forming screws eliminate this risk entirely in suitable materials.
Material displacement mechanics
Forming screws cold-work the substrate. The displaced material is compacted around the thread flanks, increasing local hardness and creating a thread that is often stronger than the parent hole.
Effect on joint performance
Formed threads typically exhibit higher strip-out torque, better vibration resistance, and superior clamp load retention. However, they require controlled pilot hole size and ductile substrates — brittle materials crack rather than flow.
Material-Specific Guidance
Sheet metal
Use trilobular or TAPTITE-style forming screws for ductile sheet metal. Pilot hole diameter is critical. For thin gauges (<1.5 mm), consider extruded holes to increase engagement length.
Thicker ductile metals
Forming screws work well in aluminum, low-carbon steel, and brass. Avoid forming in high-strength steel (>HRC 28) — drive torques become excessive. Switch to cutting (Type F, Type 23) for harder metals.
Thermoplastics
Use REMFORM-style, DELTA PT, or PT-style forming screws. Asymmetric thread profiles reduce radial hoop stress on the boss. Boss wall thickness and outer diameter are critical design inputs.
Brittle / thermoset materials
Brittle substrates cannot accommodate the radial stress of forming. Use thread cutting screws (Type 23, Type 25) which remove material rather than displacing it. Test for cracking at the extremes of your hole tolerance range.
Stainless steel
Thread cutting is generally preferred in stainless due to the material’s work-hardening behavior. Forming screws generate high drive torques and risk galling. If forming is required, use dedicated forming grades with controlled lubrication.
Die cast zinc / cast aluminum
Die castings have variable local hardness and porosity. Cutting screws are commonly used. If forming, use conservative pilot hole sizing toward the upper end of the range. Pre-testing for strip-out is strongly advised.
Hole Design Guidance
Pilot hole importance
Pilot hole diameter is the single most critical variable for forming screws. Too tight: excessive drive torque, potential strip-out or boss cracking. Too loose: insufficient thread engagement, reduced clamp load. Always test at both extremes.
Molded vs drilled holes
Molded holes can have draft angle, surface skin differences, and tolerance variation. Drilled holes cut through any surface skin. Both work — pilot hole recommendations typically apply to molded holes unless otherwise specified.
Punched vs drilled holes in metal
Punched holes have a work-hardened edge and slight taper. Insert from the punch-entry side where possible. Drilled holes provide more consistent geometry. Either is compatible with forming and cutting screws.
Engagement length
Strip-out strength increases with engagement length. For metal, minimum engagement of 1× nominal diameter is common. For plastics, follow boss geometry guidelines. Thin sheet metal may require extruded holes to achieve sufficient engagement.
Common Failure Modes
Excessive drive torque
Cause: pilot hole too small, material too hard, no lubrication. Result: bit cam-out, head failure, or cross-threading. Fix: verify pilot hole diameter, re-evaluate material compatibility, add lubrication.
Boss cracking (plastics)
Cause: pilot hole too small, boss OD insufficient, material too brittle. Fix: increase boss OD, increase pilot hole toward upper end of range, switch to lower-radial-stress form (REMFORM-style).
Strip-out on assembly
Cause: pilot hole too large, insufficient engagement depth, oversized screw tolerance. Fix: reduce pilot hole diameter, increase boss height or material thickness, verify incoming dimensions against spec.
Chips causing assembly issues
Cutting screws always generate chips. In blind holes, design chip relief volume below the screw tip. Consider chip contamination risk in electronics assemblies — forming screws are strongly preferred in such cases.
Validation Guidance
Drive torque testing
Measure drive torque across the full pilot hole and screw tolerance range. Document the torque window: prevailing torque on entry, seating torque, and strip-out torque. Ratio of strip-to-drive should be >2× as a practical minimum.
Strip-to-failure testing
Apply monotonic torque to failure. Record peak torque and failure mode. Test at minimum pilot hole and maximum screw size for worst-case. Repeat with aged/heat-cycled substrates for plastics.
Repeated assembly testing
If service reusability is required, test installation and removal cycles to the expected service count plus a margin. Record torque change per cycle. Forming screws in metal generally sustain multiple cycles well; plastics degrade more quickly.
Environmental validation
For plastics in thermal cycling applications, validate at temperature extremes. Plastics creep under sustained clamp load. For metal assemblies in corrosive environments, evaluate galvanic compatibility and coating impact on drive/strip torque.

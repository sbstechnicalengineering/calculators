Interpretation and Warnings

    Load Sharing Concept

    The joint constant estimates the fraction of external axial load added to the bolt while the joint remains clamped.

    C = kb / (kb + km)

    Lower C usually means the bolt sees less cyclic load. This is often achieved with a more elastic bolt and a stiffer clamped joint.

    Best Practice Guide

    Training / educational

    Why joint stiffness matters

    A preloaded bolted joint behaves like two springs: the bolt is stretched and the clamped members are compressed. When an external axial load is applied, only part of that load should increase bolt tension. The remainder unloads the clamped members.

    • A stiffer clamped stack generally lowers the fraction of load added to the bolt.
    • A longer, more elastic bolt can reduce cyclic bolt stress.
    • Soft gaskets, plastics, thin flanges, or embedment can increase joint movement and preload loss.

    Preload and torque control

    Torque is an indirect way to create preload. Most of the applied torque is consumed by thread and bearing friction, so preload scatter can be large.

    • Use realistic nut factor values for the actual coating, lubrication, washer, and bearing surface.
    • Consider torque-angle, yield-control, direct tension indicating washers, or measured elongation for critical joints.
    • Account for relaxation, embedment, gasket creep, paint compression, and thermal effects.

    Fatigue design logic

    Fatigue risk is driven by cyclic stress range, thread root stress concentration, mean stress, preload scatter, material, manufacturing quality, surface finish, coating, and environment.

    • Reducing bolt load range is usually more important than simply increasing bolt diameter.
    • Adequate preload helps keep the joint clamped and limits alternating bolt stress.
    • Avoid joint separation; once separation begins, bolt load can rise rapidly.

    Ways to improve joint reliability

    • Increase clamped member stiffness with thicker flanges, larger bearing area, washers, bosses, or sleeves.
    • Increase effective grip length when practical to make the bolt more elastic.
    • Use controlled preload and validated friction assumptions.
    • Minimize eccentric loading, bending, prying, and transverse slip.
    • Use appropriate locking features only after the clamp-load design is sound.

    Special caution: tapped holes and studs

    Tapped-hole joints add substrate thread strength, engagement length, material strength, and thread-form quality concerns. This estimator does not calculate thread stripping or pull-out.

    • Check internal thread shear, boss strength, and minimum engagement.
    • Be especially careful with aluminum, magnesium, castings, plastics, and inserts.
    • Consider helicoils, thread inserts, or larger engagement where fatigue and serviceability matter.

    Special caution: SEMS and washers

    SEMS assemblies and washers can change bearing friction, embedment, bearing area, prevailing torque, and preload retention.

    • Confirm whether the washer is free-spinning, conical, toothed, captive, or spring-type.
    • Do not assume a lock washer solves a fatigue or preload problem.
    • Validate torque-preload behavior with the actual washer and mating surface.

    References, Formulas and Assumptions

    Estimator basis

    Primary simplified formulas

    Fpreload = T / (K x d)
    kb = Eb x At / Leffective
    km = stiffnessFactor x kb
    C = kb / (kb + km)
    DFb = C x DFexternal
    sa = (DFb / 2) / At

    These relationships are simplified to support early design comparison and training.

    Reference names to consider

    • VDI 2230: systematic calculation of high-duty bolted joints.
    • NASA-STD-5020: requirements and definitions for threaded fastening systems in spaceflight hardware.
    • NASA / Sandia bolted joint design guidance: preload uncertainty, joint separation, and safety-factor concepts.
    • ISO 898 and SAE J429: common mechanical property references for carbon/alloy steel fasteners.
    • ASTM F606 / ISO 16047: testing and torque-tension considerations.

    What this tool does not calculate

    • Thread stripping or pull-out.
    • Bending, prying, eccentric loads, or transverse slip.
    • Thermal expansion mismatch.
    • Detailed gasket creep or viscoelastic behavior.
    • Goodman, Soderberg, Gerber, or VDI 2230 fatigue proof.
    • Reliability, scatter, surface defect, coating, corrosion, or hydrogen embrittlement effects.

    Recommended use

    • Use the estimator to compare alternatives and identify risk drivers.
    • Use validated material data, actual torque-tension testing, and customer-approved standards for final decisions.
    • Escalate critical safety, fatigue, pressure, lifting, vehicle, aerospace, or regulatory joints for formal engineering review.

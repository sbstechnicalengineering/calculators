Best Practice Guide

1. Treat this as a screening tool, not a certificate

Compliance cannot be determined absolutely from a generic material and finish description. A part described as “zinc plated” may be trivalent, hexavalent, sealed, dyed, topcoated, or supplied under different regional chemistries. The tool should guide the next question: what evidence is needed?

2. RoHS: evaluate homogeneous materials

RoHS is commonly misunderstood as a total-part-weight calculation. The practical screening question is whether each homogeneous material in the article exceeds the restricted substance threshold. For fasteners, the base metal, plating layer, chromate conversion coating, sealers, lubricants, plastic washers, and adhesives may need separate consideration.

Important fastener note: A thin zinc layer plus hexavalent chromate conversion coating, for example 8 µm on a steel washer, does not automatically make the overall part non-RoHS simply because the washer has high surface area relative to weight. The concern is not only total coating weight divided by total part weight. The correct review should consider the restricted substance concentration in the relevant homogeneous material or coating layer, the chemistry used, any exemptions, and supplier evidence.

3. Trivalent vs. hexavalent chromate

  • Trivalent chromate / Cr(III): generally preferred for RoHS screening and modern automotive/industrial requirements.
  • Hexavalent chromate / Cr(VI): higher regulatory and customer acceptance risk. Do not assume failure without documentation, but require specific supplier confirmation and, when needed, test evidence.
  • Unknown yellow chromate: treat as higher risk until confirmed. “Yellow zinc” historically may imply hexavalent chemistry, but color alone is not enough proof.

4. REACH: check SVHC and restrictions

For REACH, the screening should ask whether any SVHC is present above the reporting threshold in the article, and whether Annex XVII restrictions apply. Brass, leaded steel, plasticizers, PFAS-containing materials, adhesives, sealers, and coatings deserve extra attention.

5. Prop 65: listed substance does not always equal warning

This tool flags whether a material or coating may contain a Prop 65 listed substance. Prop 65 warning decisions depend on exposure, intended use, route of exposure, safe harbor levels when available, and whether the product can reach California consumers or workers.

6. Manufacturer vs. distributor vs. end user responsibilities

RoleTypical ResponsibilityRecommended Evidence
Manufacturer / PlaterKnows actual chemistry, process, coating, and material formulation.RoHS declaration, REACH SVHC declaration, Prop 65 statement, SDS where applicable, test reports, full material disclosure.
DistributorCollects and transmits evidence; avoids over-certifying beyond supplier data.Supplier declarations, controlled part revision, lot traceability, customer-facing compliance statement with limitations.
End User / OEMDefines market, application, exposure conditions, customer-specific thresholds, and final acceptability.Approved supplier documentation, regulatory review, IMDS/CAMDS/CDX/SCIP submission where required.

7. High-risk fastener examples

  • Cadmium plated aerospace or legacy parts.
  • Yellow zinc with unknown chromate chemistry.
  • Leaded brass, free-machining brass, and some leaded steels.
  • PVC, soft plastics, rubber, or polymer washers with unknown plasticizers.
  • Nickel-plated parts with possible skin contact or California consumer exposure.
  • PFAS/PTFE-containing lubricants, fluoropolymer coatings, or sealers where customer policy restricts them.

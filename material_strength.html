Strength Metric Definitions
Ultimate Tensile Strength
UTS / Tensile Strength
The maximum stress a material can withstand before fracturing under a sustained tensile load. This is the peak of the stress-strain curve. UTS is the most commonly cited fastener strength value and is used to compare grades across standards. Values are expressed in PSI (imperial) or MPa (metric). Note: a fastener should never be loaded to its UTS in service.
Yield Strength
0.2% Offset Proof Strength
The stress at which a material begins to permanently deform (plastically). Below the yield point, deformation is elastic and reversible. Above it, the fastener takes a permanent set and will not return to its original length. Yield strength is typically 70 to 90% of UTS for steel fasteners. Properly designed joints keep working loads below the yield point.
Proof Load
Proof Stress / Proof Strength
The maximum tensile force a fastener must withstand without permanent deformation, as defined by the governing standard. Proof load is the specified load used during production testing. It is typically 85 to 95% of yield strength. When a fastener passes proof load testing it is guaranteed not to take a permanent set. This is the practical design limit for preloaded joints.
Hardness
HRC / HRB (Rockwell Scale)
A measure of a material's resistance to localized plastic deformation (indentation). For fasteners, hardness correlates strongly with tensile strength. The Rockwell C scale (HRC) is used for hardened steels (grades 8.8 and above). Rockwell B (HRB) is used for softer materials. Standards define both minimum and maximum hardness to prevent brittleness in high-strength grades. Hardness testing is a quick production quality check.
Relationship summary: Proof Load is less than Yield Strength, which is less than UTS. In a properly preloaded joint, the installed clamp force (preload) targets approximately 70 to 75% of the proof load. This hierarchy ensures the joint remains elastic under service loads with an appropriate safety margin.
Material Profiles: Pros, Cons, and Typical Applications
Carbon / Alloy Steel
SAE Gr2 to Gr8 / ISO 4.6 to 12.9
Advantages
  • Highest strength-to-cost ratio
  • Wide range of grades available
  • Excellent machinability
  • Well-established standards
  • Easy to plate and coat
Limitations
  • Susceptible to corrosion without coating
  • High-strength grades prone to hydrogen embrittlement
  • Magnetic (can be a constraint)
  • Coating adds lead time and cost
Best for: General structural, automotive, and industrial applications where coating is acceptable. Grade 8 / 10.9 and 12.9 for high-load bolted joints.
Stainless Steel
ASTM F593 / A193 / ISO 3506
Advantages
  • Excellent corrosion resistance
  • Non-magnetic (austenitic grades)
  • Good appearance, no plating needed
  • Food-safe (316 grade)
  • Wide chemical resistance
Limitations
  • Lower strength than alloy steel (most grades)
  • Prone to galling under torque
  • Higher cost than carbon steel
  • Chloride stress corrosion in 304
Best for: Marine, chemical, food processing, and outdoor applications. Use anti-galling compound during installation. Specify 316 for chloride environments.
Titanium
ASTM F467 / Grade 2 and Grade 5
Advantages
  • Exceptional strength-to-weight
  • Superior corrosion resistance
  • Non-magnetic
  • Biocompatible (medical use)
  • Excellent fatigue resistance
Limitations
  • Very high cost
  • Difficult to machine
  • Galling risk under torque
  • Limited availability of sizes
  • Lower modulus than steel
Best for: Aerospace, motorsport, medical implants, and high-performance marine where weight savings and corrosion immunity justify premium cost. Always use lubricant to prevent galling.
Aluminum
ASTM B211 / 6061-T6 and 7075-T6
Advantages
  • Very lightweight (1/3 density of steel)
  • Natural oxide corrosion protection
  • Non-magnetic
  • Good thermal conductivity
  • No hydrogen embrittlement risk
Limitations
  • Low strength vs steel
  • Soft head can strip easily
  • Galvanic corrosion with steel
  • Not suitable for high-temp
  • Creep under sustained load
Best for: Aerospace brackets, electronics enclosures, bicycle and motorsport components where weight is critical. Avoid contact with dissimilar metals without isolation. Use low torque values.
Brass
ASTM B16 / CuZn37 and CuZn36Pb3
Advantages
  • Excellent corrosion resistance
  • Non-magnetic and non-sparking
  • Good electrical conductivity
  • Easy to machine (Pb grade)
  • Attractive appearance
Limitations
  • Low strength vs steel
  • Dezincification in some environments
  • Stress corrosion cracking risk
  • High cost vs steel
  • Not suitable for high loads
Best for: Electrical connections, plumbing fittings, decorative hardware, marine instruments, and explosive atmospheres where non-sparking is required.
Free-Machining Steel
ASTM A108 / AISI 12L14
Advantages
  • Excellent machinability
  • High-volume turned part efficiency
  • Good surface finish
  • Moderate strength
  • Tight dimensional tolerances
Limitations
  • Not weldable (Pb content)
  • Corrosion protection required
  • Lower ductility than plain steel
  • RoHS concerns (Pb)
  • Not for structural use
Best for: Precision machined components, instrumentation fittings, bushings, and standoffs where dimensional accuracy and surface finish are priorities and structural loading is low.
Key Engineering Considerations When Selecting Fastener Material
01
Load Type and Magnitude
Define whether loads are static, dynamic (fatigue), or impact. High-strength grades (Gr8, 10.9, 12.9) resist static loads well but may be susceptible to hydrogen embrittlement or stress corrosion cracking. Fatigue-critical joints may benefit from lower preload and finer thread pitch.
02
Operating Environment
Consider temperature range, humidity, chemical exposure, and UV. Carbon steel requires coating in wet environments. Stainless 316 outperforms 304 in chloride environments. Titanium and certain nickel alloys are preferred for extreme chemical duty. Elevated temperature reduces strength in most materials.
03
Galvanic Compatibility
When two dissimilar metals are in contact in an electrolyte, the less noble metal corrodes accelerated. Avoid aluminum fasteners with carbon steel structures. Stainless fasteners in carbon steel structures can accelerate steel corrosion. Use isolation washers or select compatible material pairs.
04
Hydrogen Embrittlement Risk
High-strength steel fasteners (above 1040 MPa / Grade 8 and above) are susceptible to hydrogen embrittlement, particularly after electroplating. Risk increases with hardness. Consider mechanical zinc plating, hot-dip galvanizing, or alternate materials for critical high-strength applications.
05
Galling in Stainless and Titanium
Austenitic stainless and titanium are prone to galling (cold welding under torque) which can seize threads irreversibly. Always use a suitable anti-galling lubricant (nickel-based for SS, molybdenum disulfide for Ti). Consider a nut of different alloy grade to reduce galling risk.
06
Nut and Mating Thread Compatibility
Always match nut grade to bolt grade. Using an understrength nut causes thread stripping before bolt fracture. The joint must fail in the intended mode. Hardened washers are required under bolt heads and nuts for Grade 8, 10.9, and 12.9 to prevent embedment and maintain preload.
07
Weight Constraints
When mass is critical (aerospace, motorsport, portable equipment), compare specific strength: UTS divided by density. Titanium Grade 5 and high-strength aluminum approach the specific strength of alloy steel at a fraction of the weight. Always verify absolute load capacity still meets requirements.
08
Regulatory and Standards Compliance
Pressure vessel, aerospace, food, and medical applications require certified fasteners from traceable sources with full material certifications (certs). Verify that your selected grade and standard are acceptable to the governing code (ASME, AS/NZS, EN, MIL-SPEC). Never substitute grades without engineering review.
09
Cost and Availability
Balance performance requirements against total cost including material, coating, installation time, and maintenance. Carbon steel Grade 5 or 8 with hot-dip galvanizing often gives better total lifecycle value than stainless in many outdoor structural applications. Titanium costs 10 to 30 times more than alloy steel per fastener.

Best Practice: Stainless Steel Thread Galling

What Is Galling?

Galling is a severe form of adhesive wear. In threaded fasteners, it happens when the male and female thread flanks slide against each other under high pressure and begin to transfer material. As tightening continues, the transferred material builds up, friction rises, heat increases, and the threads can seize. In practice, the user may see a nut stop suddenly, a bolt twist off, thread stripping, or hardware that cannot be removed without damage.

Why Stainless Steel Is More Prone

Austenitic stainless grades such as 18-8, 304, 316, A2, and A4 rely on a thin passive oxide film for corrosion resistance. During tightening, local high spots on the thread flanks can break through this film. Once clean metal touches clean metal under pressure, adhesion can occur. Stainless steels also tend to work harden, so the contact can become more severe as the joint continues to turn.

It Is Not Always a Bad Thread

Galling is often blamed on an out-of-spec bolt or nut, but many galling events occur with parts that pass dimensional inspection. The root cause is usually a combination of material pairing, surface condition, speed, heat, lubrication, and thread quality. This is why the same part may assemble correctly many times and then suddenly seize in a specific batch, station, or installation method.

The Mechanism

Thread tightening creates sliding contact. If pressure is high, lubrication is absent, speed is too fast, or thread flanks are rough, surface asperities can shear. The protective oxide layer is wiped away and small fragments weld or transfer between surfaces. This debris further increases friction, which creates more heat and more adhesion. The process can accelerate quickly and feel like the fastener locked up.

Speed and Heat Matter

Fast power tools and impact wrenches can create frictional heat faster than it can dissipate. Lower speed gives the thread interface more time to move without overheating. When galling is suspected, one of the first trials should be reduced RPM or hand installation, especially for stainless-on-stainless joints.

Material Pairing

Same-grade stainless-on-stainless contact is higher risk because the mating surfaces have similar hardness, chemistry, and work-hardening behavior. Mixed compatible stainless grades, such as 304 with 316 where permitted, can reduce seizure risk. For highly critical assemblies, also review property class, corrosion resistance, magnetic requirements, and customer material specifications before changing grades.

Thread Form and Fit

Coarse threads are usually more forgiving than fine threads because they have more clearance and are less sensitive to small handling marks or contamination. Fine and extra-fine threads have more flank engagement and can be more sensitive to friction. Tight fits, damaged lead threads, burrs, and poor thread starts all raise galling risk.

Thread Surface Quality

Rolled external threads are generally smoother than cut external threads. Female threads in nuts and tapped holes are often cut, so tap sharpness, tapping speed, chip control, and cleanliness matter. A dull tap or rough tapping operation can create rough internal thread flanks that drive galling even when the external bolt thread looks acceptable.

Lubrication and Wax

Lubrication is the most practical mitigation in many production environments. Anti-seize, moly, graphite, specialty anti-galling compounds, and wax patch/dry film wax can reduce friction and heat. Wax patch/dry film wax is often cost effective for production fasteners and can be applied consistently. Optional UV dye can support visual or process detection of waxed versus non-waxed parts.

Lubricate the Interface

Lubricating the bolt alone may help, but galling occurs at the interface. Where the application allows, control lubrication on both the male thread and the nut or tapped hole. If the joint is used in food processing, cleanroom, painted, coated, oxygen service, or chemically sensitive applications, lubricant compatibility must be reviewed before use.

Torque and Tension Impact

Lubrication changes clamp load for a given torque. As a crude educational estimate, dry/passivated stainless may have a nut factor around K = 0.25 to 0.35, while waxed or lubricated stainless may be around K = 0.15 to 0.22. This can reduce torque required for the same clamp load by about 25 to 45 percent. Any lubrication change should trigger torque validation.

Do Not Reuse Damaged Hardware

Threads that have already galled, partially seized, or been forced through a rough joint should not be reused as if they were new. Reuse can carry embedded debris, damaged flanks, and work-hardened areas into the next assembly. Cleanliness, packaging, and handling controls are part of galling prevention.

Practical Troubleshooting Sequence

Start with the lowest-cost corrective actions: slow the installation speed, confirm the correct thread start, inspect for burrs or contamination, check the nut or tapped hole quality, and add approved lubrication or wax patch/dry film wax. If risk remains high, consider mixed compatible stainless grades, rolled external threads, looser standard fit where allowed, alternate coatings or solid film lubricants, and production torque validation.

Recommended Mitigation Hierarchy Control speed and installation RPM, then clean and inspect threads, then add approved lubricant or wax patch, then lubricate both mating surfaces where allowed, then consider mixed stainless grades, and finally validate torque and clamp load.

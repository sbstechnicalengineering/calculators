Understanding Galvanic Corrosion

What Is Galvanic Corrosion?

Galvanic corrosion occurs when two dissimilar metals are in electrical contact in the presence of an electrolyte (moisture, water, or a conductive solution). The more active metal (anode) corrodes preferentially while the more noble metal (cathode) is protected. The driving force is the difference in electrochemical potential between the two metals.

Key principle: The greater the potential difference between two metals, the higher the galvanic corrosion risk. Metals close together in the galvanic series are generally compatible; metals far apart are not.

The Three Conditions Required

All three conditions must be present simultaneously for galvanic corrosion to occur. Eliminating any one of them stops the reaction.

  • Two dissimilar metals with different electrochemical potentials
  • Electrical contact between the two metals (direct metal-to-metal or via a conductor)
  • An electrolyte bridging the two metals (moisture, condensation, seawater, process fluid)

Design implication: Isolation (dielectric washers, coatings, sealants) works by interrupting electrical contact or excluding the electrolyte.

Design Guidelines for Fastener Selection

Potential Difference Thresholds

These are general industry guidelines. Adjust for severity of environment using the environment multiplier in the Calculator tab.

  • Less than 150 mV (adjusted): Low risk. Compatible for most applications with standard design practices.
  • 150 to 500 mV (adjusted): Moderate risk. Use protective coatings, isolation, or regular inspection.
  • Greater than 500 mV (adjusted): High risk. Avoid if possible; if unavoidable, implement full isolation and frequent inspection.

Fastener Material Hierarchy

When selecting a fastener material for a given substrate, preference should follow this hierarchy:

  • Same material as substrate (zero potential difference, no galvanic reaction)
  • Similar alloy family (small potential difference, low risk)
  • More noble than substrate (fastener becomes cathode and is protected; substrate may corrode at joint)
  • More active than substrate (fastener corrodes preferentially; often acceptable with sacrificial design intent)

Avoid: A large active fastener in contact with a small noble substrate. The fastener corrodes rapidly due to unfavorable area ratio (small anode, large cathode).

Isolation Techniques

  • Dielectric washers: Nylon, PTFE, or neoprene washers break electrical contact at the bolt hole interface.
  • Dielectric sleeves: Non-conductive sleeves around the bolt shank prevent contact through the hole.
  • Sealants and coatings: Epoxy, zinc-rich primer, or paste compound applied at the interface excludes the electrolyte.
  • Anodizing or passivation: Surface treatments that increase corrosion resistance of the fastener or substrate.
  • Plating: Zinc, cadmium, or nickel plating on carbon steel fasteners changes their effective galvanic potential.

Best result: Combine electrical isolation (washers and sleeves) with electrolyte exclusion (sealant) for the most reliable protection in aggressive environments.

Environment Severity Factors

  • Dry indoor: Minimal moisture. Galvanic activity is suppressed. Low-risk pairings acceptable without special measures.
  • Humid indoor: Condensation possible. Moderate pairings require monitoring.
  • Outdoor: Rain, dew, pollutants. Use coatings or isolation for any moderate or high risk pairing.
  • Marine (salt spray): Highly conductive electrolyte. Dramatically accelerates galvanic reaction. Prefer same-family metals or titanium fasteners.
  • Submerged: Continuous electrolytic contact. Maximum galvanic activity. Use same-material or noble fasteners with full isolation and cathodic protection where feasible.

Common Compatible Pairings for Fasteners

  • Stainless Steel 304 or 316 fasteners in aluminum structure (moderate risk outdoors; use isolators in marine)
  • Stainless Steel 316 fasteners in stainless steel structure (near-zero risk)
  • Titanium fasteners in titanium or stainless structure (near-zero risk, suitable for marine)
  • Zinc-plated carbon steel fasteners in carbon steel structure (low risk; zinc provides sacrificial protection)
  • Silicon bronze fasteners in copper or bronze structure (low risk; traditional marine hardware)

Inspection and Maintenance

  • Inspect joints in aggressive environments at least annually (or per applicable specification).
  • Look for white powdery deposits (aluminum or zinc corrosion products), red rust staining, pitting, or crevice attack at the interface.
  • Replace corroded fasteners before loss of clamping force occurs.
  • Re-apply sealant if the joint has been disturbed or if the original coating shows signs of breakdown.
  • Document material pairings in maintenance records to guide future inspection intervals.

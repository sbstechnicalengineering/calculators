Best Practice Guide

This guide explains the common decision factors when comparing machining, cold forming, or a hybrid process for non-standard metal parts such as pins, bushings, spacers, standoffs, inserts, shafts, and fittings.

Decision Factor Machining Usually Looks Better When... Cold Forming Usually Looks Better When...
Run rate / EAUVolume is low, demand is uncertain, or the part is still in development.Volume is high enough to amortize tooling and process development.
Lead timeParts are needed quickly, especially under 2 weeks.There is enough time for tooling, sampling, validation, and PPAP if required.
PrecisionTolerances are tight, datums are critical, or geometry requires controlled machining surfaces.Tolerances are general to moderate and features can be formed repeatably.
Scrap / material wasteThe machined shape is close to bar stock or raw material size.The machined version removes significant material, especially on heavier parts.
GeometryThe part has cross holes, off-center features, undercuts, deep internal threads, or sharp details.The part is round, axisymmetric, headed, stepped, or externally threaded.
Design changesThe print is still changing or customer approval is not final.The design is stable and production requirements are understood.

Precision and Tolerance

  • General tolerance around plus or minus 0.25 mm / 0.010 in is often compatible with cold forming.
  • Moderate tolerance around plus or minus 0.10 mm / 0.004 in may work for either process depending on the feature.
  • Tight tolerance around plus or minus 0.05 mm / 0.002 in may require machining or secondary finishing.
  • Very tight tolerance under plus or minus 0.025 mm / 0.001 in usually needs machining, grinding, or a highly controlled secondary operation.

Run Rate and Cost

  • Machining often wins at low volume because it avoids dedicated forming tooling.
  • Cold forming often wins at high volume because the cycle time and material utilization can reduce piece price.
  • Tooling cost should be considered against expected annual usage and program life.
  • For PPAP or production launch, process stability may matter as much as the initial unit price.

Scrap and Material Utilization

  • Machining from bar stock can create high scrap when the final shape is much smaller than the starting material.
  • Cold forming can move material into shape instead of cutting it away.
  • Heavier parts with large shoulders, heads, or diameter changes may deserve a cold forming review.
  • Material savings are especially important for stainless, alloy steel, brass, copper alloys, and other expensive materials.

Threads and Strength

  • Rolled external threads can be attractive for production because they are fast and can improve thread surface condition.
  • Cut threads may be preferred for low volume, prototypes, or complex machined features.
  • Internal threads often require tapping or thread forming as a secondary operation.
  • Weak thread engagement, short thread length, or soft mating material should be reviewed carefully regardless of manufacturing method.

Cold Forming Is Often Attractive For

  • Simple pins and headed pins.
  • Stepped shafts and shoulders.
  • Spacers, bushings, inserts, and standoffs with production volume.
  • Externally threaded specials where thread rolling is practical.
  • Parts currently machined with high material waste.

Machining Is Often Attractive For

  • Prototypes and development parts.
  • Parts needed quickly.
  • Low annual volume or uncertain demand.
  • Complex features such as cross holes, off-center geometry, grooves, undercuts, and tight concentricity.
  • Critical bearing, sealing, or sliding surfaces.

Hybrid Process: Cold Formed Blank plus Secondary Machining

  • A hybrid route may be best when the basic shape is formable but critical features still need machining.
  • Examples include cold formed blank plus drilling, tapping, grinding, broaching, or finish machining.
  • This approach may reduce material waste while still meeting tighter print requirements.
  • A print review is needed to decide whether the added handling and secondary operations still make economic sense.

Information Needed for a Real Review

  • Part print, model, or clear photo.
  • EAU and expected program life.
  • Material, grade, and property class such as SAE J429 or ISO 898.
  • Critical dimensions and tolerances.
  • Required finish, coating, heat treatment, and PPAP level if applicable.

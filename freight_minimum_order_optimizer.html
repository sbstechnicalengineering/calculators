Recommendation Notes

Best Practice Theory

1. Optimize total cost, not just piece price. A lower unit price can be offset by higher freight, larger MOQ, slower turns, and more cash tied up in stock.

2. EOQ is a useful starting point. Economic Order Quantity estimates the order size that minimizes the combined cost of placing orders and carrying inventory. It works best when demand, cost, and lead time are reasonably stable.

3. MOQ must be tested against real demand. A supplier MOQ that exceeds annual or practical demand may create obsolete inventory, excess cash exposure, and poor inventory turns.

4. Freight and inventory are connected. Frequent small shipments may reduce inventory but increase freight and admin cost. Large shipments may lower freight per piece but increase holding cost and risk.

5. Reorder logic still matters. Reorder point should cover expected demand during lead time plus safety stock for variation in demand, late deliveries, or quality disruptions.

Practical SBS Review Checklist

Use ocean or consolidated freight where the forecast is stable and lead time can be planned.

Use air freight selectively for launch support, quality containment, or emergency recovery, not as the normal replenishment method unless justified by value and risk.

Challenge MOQ when it drives excess stock, especially on low-usage, service, or end-of-life parts.

Look for part-family consolidation where similar parts can share supplier, packaging, freight lane, or replenishment cadence.

Review current ROP, ROQ, on-hand, and turns together. Each number can look reasonable alone but create waste when combined incorrectly.

Sources and References

  • Allianz Trade, Economic Order Quantity. Explains EOQ principles, including the balance between ordering cost and holding cost.
  • Corporate Finance Institute, EOQ Formula and Guide. Defines EOQ components: annual demand, order cost, unit cost, and holding cost.
  • inFlow Inventory, Reorder Point Formula. Describes reorder point as average daily demand multiplied by lead time plus safety stock.
  • Transport Geography, Total Logistics Costs Tradeoff. Describes total logistics cost as including transportation, warehousing, inventory carrying, administration, and order-processing costs.
  • UC Berkeley Institute of Transportation Studies. Discusses tradeoffs between transportation, inventory, and production setup costs in freight strategy.

Hardness and Mechanical Properties

Understanding hardness and its relationship to mechanical properties is essential for fastener specification, incoming inspection, and failure analysis. This guide covers the major hardness scales, their correlations, and practical guidance for common fastener grades and property classes.

Why Hardness Matters for Fasteners

Hardness is one of the most commonly measured mechanical properties of a fastener because it provides a fast, non-destructive indicator of tensile strength, yield strength, and wear resistance. For carbon and alloy steel fasteners, ASTM E140 provides standardized conversion tables between hardness scales, and there is a well-established linear correlation between Brinell hardness and tensile strength: approximately Tensile (MPa) = 3.45 x HB for carbon steels.

Fastener standards specify hardness ranges rather than exact values because the same nominal grade can be produced from various alloy steels and heat treatment processes. The hardness range ensures the finished fastener falls within the required strength band regardless of the specific material or process used.

Hydrogen embrittlement is the most critical hardness-related failure mode. Electroplated coatings on fasteners harder than approximately 39 HRC (Property Class 10.9 and above, approximately 375 HB, 1270 MPa tensile) require mandatory post-plate bake-out at 375 degrees F for a minimum of 4 hours to drive off absorbed hydrogen. Failure to bake can result in delayed fracture under service loads with no visible warning.

Key Standards for Fastener Mechanical Properties

ISO 898-1
Mechanical properties of fasteners made of carbon steel and alloy steel. Covers metric property classes 4.6 through 12.9 including hardness, tensile, and proof load requirements.
SAE J429
Mechanical and material requirements for externally threaded fasteners. Covers SAE Grades 2, 5, 7, and 8 with hardness and tensile requirements.
ASTM A354
Quenched and tempered alloy steel bolts, studs, and other externally threaded fasteners. Covers Grades BC and BD with hardness and mechanical property requirements.
ASTM A490
High-strength structural bolts for structural steel joints. Hardness limits and mechanical requirements for heavy hex structural bolts.
ASTM F1554
Anchor bolts, grades 36, 55, and 105. Covers mechanical property requirements including tensile, yield, and hardness specifications for anchor rod applications.

Understanding Hardness Scales

Brinell (HB): The most widely reported hardness scale for fastener inspection. Uses a 10mm hardened ball indenter under 3000 kg load. Best suited for medium and high hardness steels. Has a direct approximate linear relationship to tensile strength for carbon steels. The ASTM E140 standard provides the official conversion table between Brinell and other scales.

Rockwell C (HRC): Used for high-strength, hardened steel fasteners (PC 10.9, PC 12.9, SAE Grade 8). Uses a diamond cone indenter. The maximum HRC limit (typically 39 to 44 depending on grade) is as critical as the minimum, because over-hardened fasteners are susceptible to hydrogen embrittlement and stress corrosion cracking.

Rockwell B (HRB): Used for lower strength fasteners and softer materials. Uses a 1/16 inch ball indenter. Applicable for SAE Grade 2, PC 4.6, PC 4.8, and austenitic stainless steel. Scale is valid approximately 60 to 100 HRB.

Vickers (HV): High precision test using a diamond pyramid indenter. Common in Europe and for thin sections or case-hardened parts. Numerically similar to Brinell at low hardness but diverges at higher values. Used in ISO 898-1 for metric property class verification.

Important note on stainless steel: Standard hardness-to-tensile conversion tables based on ASTM E140 do NOT apply to austenitic stainless steel (A2, A4). Strength is achieved by cold working, not heat treatment, and the correlation between hardness and tensile strength is not reliable for these materials.

Maximum Hardness Limits and Hydrogen Embrittlement

Maximum hardness limits are specified in fastener standards to control hydrogen embrittlement (HE) susceptibility. The generally accepted threshold for significant HE risk in electroplated fasteners is 39 HRC (approximately 375 HB, 1270 MPa tensile). Above this level, post-plate bake-out is mandatory per ASTM F1941 and SAE/USCAR-5. Above 44 HRC, electroplating is generally not recommended regardless of bake-out.

For high-strength fasteners (PC 10.9, PC 12.9, SAE Grade 8, ASTM A354 BD, ASTM A490), specify mechanical zinc plating, Geomet, Dacromet, or other low-hydrogen-risk coating processes wherever possible to eliminate HE risk at the source.

ASTM A490 structural bolts explicitly prohibit zinc or cadmium electroplating per AISC specification due to HE risk at the high hardness levels associated with this grade.

Hardness Testing Best Practices

Test location: For bolt hardness, testing is typically performed on the head bearing surface or a cross-section of the shank. Heads are often softer than shanks due to cold working during the heading process. Always check the applicable standard for the required test location. ISO 898-1 specifies that Vickers measurements be taken at approximately one diameter from the bolt end on the shank cross-section.

Surface preparation: All hardness tests require a clean, flat, smooth surface. Threads, corrosion, scale, plating, or coatings must be removed. A rough or curved test surface produces artificially low readings and should be rejected. For field testing, portable Brinell or Rockwell instruments require a minimum flat area and surface prep.

Conversion accuracy: Conversions between scales and to tensile strength are approximations. They are most accurate for homogeneous carbon and low-alloy steels tested in the specified hardness range. They are less accurate for case-hardened parts, stainless, or non-ferrous materials. Do not substitute hardness-converted tensile values for actual tensile test results on structural or safety-critical applications.

Incoming inspection: Hardness testing provides a fast, cost-effective screen for incoming fastener lots. Develop a sampling plan per ANSI/ASQ Z1.4 or your customer requirements. Any fastener exceeding the maximum hardness limit should trigger quarantine and full mechanical testing before acceptance, regardless of whether minimum strength is met.

Hardness Testing Standards

ISO 898-1
Specifies Vickers hardness test locations and acceptance criteria for metric property classes. Reference for minimum and maximum hardness for PC 4.6 through 12.9.
SAE J429
Specifies Rockwell hardness test requirements and limits for SAE Grades 2, 5, 7, and 8. Includes test location guidance for bolt head and body.
ASTM A354
Rockwell C hardness requirements for Grades BC and BD. Maximum 36 HRC for BC and 39 HRC for BD to limit hydrogen embrittlement risk.
ASTM A490
Hardness requirements and maximum limits for high-strength structural bolts. Prohibition of zinc electroplating due to HE risk at high hardness levels.
ASTM F1554
Mechanical and hardness requirements for anchor bolts Grades 36, 55, and 105. Includes supplemental requirements for weldability and straightness.

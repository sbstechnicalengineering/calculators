Best Practice Guide

Why drive size selection matters

Correct drive engagement helps prevent cam-out, recess rounding, stripped tools, cosmetic damage, inconsistent torque, and poor serviceability. Drive size should be treated as part of the joint design, not only as a catalog detail.

Do not assume one drive size for every screw size

The same thread size may use different drives depending on head style, dimensional standard, recess depth, head diameter, strength class, manufacturer design, and customer print requirements. Always confirm against the drawing or controlling standard.

Internal hex vs. hexalobular/Torx-style

Internal hex drives are common and easy to service, but smaller sizes can round if tool fit is poor or torque is high. Hexalobular drives generally provide better torque transfer and lower cam-out tendency when the correct bit is used.

Phillips, Pozidriv, and cross-recess drives

Phillips and Pozidriv are not interchangeable. Pozidriv bits have additional ribs and are designed for different engagement. Using the wrong bit can damage the recess and increase cam-out risk.

Slotted drives

Slotted drives are simple and visually familiar, but they are not ideal for high-speed production assembly. Bit alignment, blade width, and blade thickness are important to avoid slipping and cosmetic damage.

Square/Robertson drives

Square drives offer positive engagement and are common in wood, sheet metal, and some regional applications. Confirm recess size carefully because close sizes may appear similar but fit differently.

Tamper-resistant and specialty drives

Pin-in hex, pin-in hexalobular, spanner, tri-wing, clutch, one-way, and other specialty drives should be selected only when service control or tamper resistance is required. Confirm tool availability before release.

Assembly guidance
  • Use a clean, unworn tool bit with full engagement.
  • Keep the tool aligned with the drive axis.
  • Do not substitute close but incorrect sizes such as T25 for T27.
  • For powered assembly, validate bit life, recess damage, torque scatter, and removal performance.
  • For coated fasteners, consider coating buildup in recesses and wrenching features.

Orange = Rivet Body alloy  |  Green = Mandrel alloy — two separate materials in every blind rivet.
Rivet Body (Shell)
Rivet Body
The hollow sleeve that deforms to clamp materials. Its alloy determines joint shear strength and corrosion resistance per IFI-114 and IFI-135.
Rivet Head
Rivet Body
The flange on the body sitting on the access side. Head style (dome, truss, countersunk, flat) governs load distribution and accessibility.
Mandrel (Stem / Nail)
Mandrel
The pull pin pulled through the body during setting. Expands the tail then snaps at the break groove per IFI-114. A small stub remains inside the body.
Mandrel Head
Mandrel
The enlarged tip that deforms the rivet body tail on the blind side. Controls blind-side bearing diameter and clamp force (IFI-110 / ISO 14588).
TWO MATERIALS IN ONE RIVET - IFI-114 / IFI-110
A blind rivet contains two different alloys. A spec such as SS316 / Steel means:
SS316 = Rivet Body - determines joint strength and corrosion resistance.
Steel = Mandrel - breaks off; a small stub remains inside the body.
For marine or chemical service, always specify all-same-alloy (body and mandrel identical) to prevent stub corrosion. See IFI-126 for sealed closed-end types.

Standard Head Styles
Dome (Round Head) - Most common. High profile, large bearing area. IFI-114 / ASME B18.30.1M / ISO 15983.
Truss / Large Flange - Extra-wide low-profile head. Best for soft or thin materials. Prevents pull-through. IFI-114.
Countersunk (Flush) - Sits flush with surface. For aerodynamic, sliding, or aesthetic applications. IFI-119 / ASME B18.1.2.
Flat Head - Low-profile with wide bearing area. Intermediate between dome and truss. IFI-114 / ISO 15983.
Rivet Types by Construction
Open End (Standard) - Break-mandrel type. Body remains open after setting. IFI-114 / ISO 15983 / ASME B18.30.1M.
Multi-Grip - One rivet covers wide grip range. Mandrel breaks at progressive grooves. IFI-116.
Peel - Body splits into petals for very wide load distribution. Ideal for fragile materials. IFI-130.
Grooved / Self-Plugging - Mandrel retained in body after setting. Higher vibration and shear resistance. IFI-119 / IFI-134.
Closed End (Sealed) - Body sealed at blind end. No water or gas ingress. IFI-126. Essential for marine applications.
Pop® Rivet vs Blind Rivet — What is the Difference?
Common Customer Clarification
Pop Rivet vs Blind Rivet vs Pop® Rivet - What is the Difference?
The answer is: there is little to no difference between Pop® rivet, pop rivet, and blind rivet. A blind rivet means you do not need access to the other side of the working piece. Also known as pop rivets because Pop® was the name of the original rivet company (Tucker Pop Rivet Company), and because of the characteristic pop sound the mandrel makes when it snaps at the break groove on installation.
Pop® Rivet (Trademark)
Pop® is a registered trademark - originally the Tucker Pop Rivet Company. Like "Hoover" for vacuums, the brand name became so dominant it entered common usage. Customers asking for "Pop rivets" almost always mean any blind rivet.
Pop Rivet (Generic)
The common informal term for any blind rivet, regardless of manufacturer. Named for the audible pop the mandrel makes when it snaps. Used interchangeably with "blind rivet" in trade and retail worldwide.
Blind Rivet (Technical)
The correct technical term per IFI-110 (Glossary of Terms Relating to Blind Rivets) and ISO 14588. "Blind" means installation from one side only - the back (blind) side is not accessible.
Other Trade and Brand Names
Avdel® / Monobolt®
Avdel is a leading manufacturer of structural blind rivets. Monobolt® and Avbolt® are structural-grade pull-through fasteners meeting IFI-134 structural specifications. Require proprietary tooling.
Bulb-Tite® / Peel Types
Trade names for large-flange peel-type blind rivets (IFI-130). The mandrel causes the body to peel into petals, creating a very large blind-side footprint. Ideal for thin or soft base materials.
Cherry® / CherryMAX®
Cherry Aerospace produces aerospace-grade blind fasteners including grooved/self-plugging types per IFI-119 and IFI-134. CherryMAX® is specified in military and civil aircraft programmes.
Frequently Asked Questions
Which IFI standard applies to my rivet type?
IFI-114 covers standard open-end break-mandrel blind rivets. IFI-116 covers multi-grip types. IFI-117 covers pull-through mandrel types. IFI-126 covers closed-end sealed types. IFI-119 and IFI-134 cover structural self-plugging types. IFI-110 defines all terminology. IFI-135 defines mechanical testing procedures. IFI-137 covers inspection and quality assurance. ISO 14588 provides international terminology definitions. ASME B18.30.1M covers metric open-end blind rivets.
Does the mandrel material affect corrosion performance?
Yes - and this is frequently overlooked. A standard stainless body with a steel mandrel leaves a small carbon steel stub inside the body after installation. In marine or wet environments this stub can corrode causing staining or joint degradation. For true corrosion resistance specify all-same-alloy rivets (SS316 body / SS316 mandrel) or specify a closed-end type per IFI-126 which seals the body and eliminates stub exposure.
What does a specification like 4.8 x 10 mean?
Standard callout format: diameter x grip length. So 4.8 x 10 means a 4.8 mm diameter rivet with an overall body length of 10 mm, suitable for a total material grip of approximately 6 to 10 mm. Always check the grip range table on the packaging per IFI-114 dimensional requirements.
What is the difference between a blind rivet and a solid rivet?
A solid rivet (ASME B18.1.1) has no mandrel and is set by deforming the shank with a bucking bar, requiring access to both sides. A blind rivet (IFI-114) is installed from one side only using a pull mandrel. Solid rivets generally achieve higher shear strength and are preferred in primary aircraft structure. Blind rivets are faster and require only single-sided access.
Rivet Washer Selection Guide
Reference: DIN 7349 — Washers for use with Blind Rivets
When to Use a Rivet Washer: Rivet washers (backup washers) are used under the rivet head or on the blind side to distribute clamp force over a larger area, prevent pull-through in soft or thin materials, and protect the workpiece surface from the rivet head.
What is a Rivet Washer?

A rivet washer is a flat annular disc placed under the rivet head (access side) or on the blind side to increase the bearing area of the fastener. Standardised under DIN 7349, they are used when:

  • The base material is soft, thin, or prone to deformation (plastics, composites, thin metal)
  • The rivet hole is oversized or elongated
  • Additional pull-through resistance is needed
  • Load must be distributed per IFI-114 edge distance rules
  • Surface protection from the rivet head is required

DIN 7349 — Standard Overview
DIN 7349 — Large Series Flat Washers
DIN 7349 specifies large-series flat washers with inner diameters matched to standard bolt and rivet nominal diameters. The OD is significantly larger than DIN 125 standard washers — ideal for distributing fastener load over a wider bearing area. Available in steel (plain, zinc-plated) and stainless grades.

Selection Rules
Inner Diameter Match
Washer ID must be +0.1 to +0.3 mm larger than rivet nominal diameter.
ID more than 1 mm larger than rivet Ø — risk of head pull-through.
ID₁ ≈ D₂ᵢᵛᵉᵗ + 0.2 mm
Outer Diameter — Load Spread
DIN 7349 OD is 3–4× the rivet diameter — suitable for most applications.
Larger OD = lower bearing stress on material surface.
OD ≥ 3 × D₂ᵢᵛᵉᵗ (DIN 7349)
Material Compatibility
Always match washer alloy to rivet body alloy to prevent galvanic corrosion.
Marine: SS316 washer with SS316 rivet. Never mix carbon steel with stainless in contact.
When NOT to Use a Washer
Countersunk (flush) head rivets — the head is recessed and a washer prevents proper seating.
Thick rigid steel base (>3 mm) — rivet head provides adequate bearing area directly.

Core Design Rules
Rivet Diameter Selection
Diameter should be 3 times the thickest sheet (IFI-114). Never smaller than the thinnest sheet.
Aerospace aluminium: D = 3t minimum per MIL-HDBK-5 / MIL-R-1.
D_min = 3 x t_max_sheet
Edge Distance and Pitch
Min edge distance: 1.5D from centerline to edge. Preferred 2D to 3D (IFI-114).
Min pitch: 3D center-to-center. Preferred 6D to 8D for fatigue joints.
e_min = 1.5D | p_min = 3D
Grip Length Selection
Grip = total stack thickness. Select a rivet whose grip range includes your stack per IFI-114.
Variable thickness: consider a multi-grip rivet per IFI-116 to reduce SKUs.
L_rivet = t_total + 1.5D
Safety Factors
Static: SF = 2.0 min. Structural: 3.0 to 4.0.
Fatigue and dynamic: SF = 4.0 to 6.0. Use grooved types (IFI-134) under vibration.
Use IFI-135 mechanical test data for verified strength values.
Material and Mandrel Selection
Match rivet body alloy to base metal. Verify galvanic compatibility between body and substrate.
Marine and chemical: specify all-same-alloy (body and mandrel identical) per IFI-114.
Consider closed-end type (IFI-126) to eliminate mandrel stub exposure entirely.
Hole Preparation and Quality
Drill to +0.003 in / +0.08 mm over nominal rivet diameter per IFI-114.
Deburr all holes. Inspect per IFI-137 quality assurance requirements.
Verify installation per IFI-135 mechanical testing procedures.

User guide
Step-by-step: running a test
1
Choose a material and grade
In the Simulation tab, pick Carbon steel, Stainless, Titanium, Brass, or Aluminum, then select a grade from the dropdown next to it.
2
Set the bolt's size and test speed
Use the Diameter and Gauge length steppers to match a real bolt, and the Speed (mm/min) stepper to set how fast the test runs — or leave them all at the defaults. Not sure what speed to use? The Best practices tab shows a recommended range calculated from your exact diameter and gauge length.
3
Click Mount bolt
The grip starts empty — nothing is under test until you attach a specimen. Click Mount bolt to lock in your current material, grade, diameter, and gauge length and attach that bolt to the grip. While a bolt is mounted, those four settings are locked; click Remove bolt first if you want to change any of them, then mount again.
4
Click Start, or drag the slider yourself
Start runs the test automatically from zero at the speed you set. Dragging the slider by hand lets you stop at any exact point to inspect it. Both are only available once a bolt is mounted.
5
Watch both panels together
The bolt visibly stretches and changes color on the left, while the stress-strain curve traces live on the right.
6
Read the stats as they update
Tensile load, stress, strain, % of yield, and % of UTS all update in real time above the panels — hover any "i" bubble for a plain-language explanation.
7
Jump to a stage or use A closer look
Once a bolt is mounted, the Jump to a stage buttons run the test straight to any stage automatically — the yield stage is marked with an orange border since that's the moment the bolt becomes permanently deformed. In the A closer look panel, hover the Previous/Next thumbnails to enlarge them, then click to jump straight there without restarting the test.
8
Click Reset to try again, or Remove bolt for a new specimen
Reset returns the current bolt to zero load so you can rerun the same test. Remove bolt detaches it entirely and unlocks material, grade, diameter, and gauge length so you can mount a different specimen.
What is this tool?
This tool simulates a standard tensile test on a bolt — pulling it in a straight line until it stretches, yields, necks, and finally breaks — so you can see exactly how a fastener's rated strength is actually measured.
Getting around the tool
User guide — you're here. Simulation — mount a bolt, then pull it and watch it stretch. Compare grades — put two bolts side by side. Best practices — what to know when reading or running a tensile test.
Mount bolt / Remove bolt
The grip starts empty. Mount bolt attaches your current material, grade, diameter, and gauge length and locks them in; Remove bolt detaches the specimen and unlocks those settings so you can set up a different one. You can mount and remove as often as you like — just remove the current bolt before changing its specs.
How to read this tool
Once mounted, drag the slider to apply load like a real tensile test. It's all one continuous pull — watch the color move from safe (green) through yield (orange) to failure (red) as the load increases.
Why this test matters
Tensile testing like this is exactly how manufacturers determine a bolt's rated strength — the Sy, Yield Strength and Su, Ultimate Tensile Strength (UTS) values used throughout this tool all come from tests just like this one.
Look for the (i) icons
Anywhere you see a small "i" bubble, just hover over it for a plain-language explanation of that term — no need to already know the jargon, and no clicking required.
Best practices
Stages of a tensile test
Every bolt goes through the same sequence on its way to fracture — modeled here on the classic specimen-with-registration-marks diagram. Head to the Simulation tab to run the test and jump straight to any of these stages.
Yield vs. ultimate strength
Yield is where permanent stretch begins; ultimate is the peak load just before failure. A bolt's usable working range sits below yield, with ultimate marking the true safety ceiling.
0.2% proof strength
Many stainless, brass, and aluminum alloys have no sharp yield point. The 0.2% offset method defines proof strength from the elastic slope, and is the standard way to report yield for these materials.
Test speed matters
Standard tensile tests pull at a controlled, slow, steady rate. Loading too fast can artificially raise the measured strength and give misleading results. Speed should scale with gauge length — and slow down slightly for larger diameters, since more cross-section needs more time to load evenly.

Necking marks the beginning of the end
Once a bolt passes ultimate strength, its cross-section starts narrowing at one point. From here, failure is a matter of when, not if.
Not all failures look the same
A ductile fracture shows visible necking and a rough, fibrous break. A brittle fracture snaps suddenly with little stretch — often a sign of the wrong material, embrittlement, or damage.
Elongation shows ductility
Percent elongation at fracture — how much the gauge section stretched before breaking — is reported alongside Sy, Yield Strength and Su, Ultimate Tensile Strength (UTS) as a standard measure of how ductile a material is.
Never reuse TTY bolts
Torque-to-yield bolts are single-use. Once a bolt has been loaded past its yield point, the shank is permanently elongated — this test shows exactly why.
Temperature affects strength
Yield and ultimate strength both shift with temperature — most materials weaken as they heat up. Standard tensile ratings assume room temperature unless a test specifically states otherwise.

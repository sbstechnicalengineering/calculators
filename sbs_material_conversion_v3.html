Understanding Material Equivalency

What is Material Equivalency?

Material equivalency refers to the practice of identifying steels from different national or international standards that share sufficiently similar chemical compositions and mechanical properties. Because different countries developed their own designation systems independently, the same or similar steel may be known by entirely different names depending on where it was produced or specified.

Equivalency is rarely exact. Even grades listed as equivalent may differ in minor alloying elements, permissible tolerances, heat treatment requirements, or testing methods. Always treat equivalencies as a starting point for engineering evaluation, not a guaranteed interchangeable substitution.

Important: Before substituting one material for another in a critical application, always consult the original standard documentation and verify that the mechanical and chemical requirements are fully met. The SBS Standards Library provides access to the full text of many relevant standards.

International Standards Bodies

Steel designations are governed by national and international standards organizations. Understanding which body issued a standard helps you interpret the grade designation and locate the authoritative specification.

Japan
JIS
Japanese Industrial Standards. Governed by JISC (Japanese Industrial Standards Committee). Prefixes: SUS (stainless), SKH (high-speed), SKD (die steel), SUM (free cutting), SUP (spring), SUJ (bearing), SUH (heat-resisting).
USA
AISI / SAE / ASTM / UNS
AISI and SAE use a 4-digit system for carbon and alloy steels. ASTM covers product-specific specifications. UNS (Unified Numbering System) provides a unified alphanumeric designation used across standards bodies.
Germany
DIN / W.Nr.
DIN (Deutsches Institut fur Normung) designations describe composition symbolically (e.g. X5CrNi18-10). The Werkstoffnummer (W.Nr. or material number) is a numeric code, e.g. 1.4301, widely used across Europe.
Europe
EN
European Standards (EN) replaced many national standards including DIN, BS, and AFNOR for EU member states. EN designations include both a symbolic name (e.g. X4CrNi18-10) and a numeric steel number (e.g. 1.4301).
United Kingdom
BS
British Standards (BS). While many BS steel standards have been superseded by EN standards, older BS designations remain in common use in drawings and specifications. Both old and new BS designations are included in this tool.
France
AFNOR / NF
AFNOR (Association Francaise de Normalisation) publishes NF (Norme Francaise) standards. French designations typically include composition indicators, e.g. Z6CN18-09 for a stainless steel with 18% Cr and 9% Ni.
Italy
UNI
Ente Italiano di Normazione (UNI) issues Italian national standards. Italian designations closely follow DIN symbolic notation. Many have been replaced by EN standards for new applications.
Russia
GOST
GOST (Gosudarstvennyy Standart) standards are used in Russia and former Soviet states. Russian designations use Cyrillic letters representing alloying elements (X = Cr, H = Ni, M = Mo, T = Ti, B = Nb).
China
GB
Guobiao (GB) standards are Chinese national standards. The newer GB designation system closely follows ISO symbolic notation. Older GB designations use a numeric system. Both new and old designations are included.
International
ISO
International Organization for Standardization. ISO steel standards (e.g. ISO/TR 15510) provide internationally agreed cross-reference tables. ISO designations are widely used as a neutral reference point for equivalency work.
Steel Material Categories

Stainless Steel

Stainless steels contain a minimum of 10.5% chromium, which forms a passive oxide layer providing corrosion resistance. They are grouped by microstructure:

  • Austenitic (300 series / SUS 3xx): Non-magnetic, excellent corrosion resistance, most common type. Examples: 304, 304L, 316, 316L. 304L and 316L are low-carbon grades used where welding is involved to prevent sensitization.
  • Ferritic (400 series / SUS 4xx): Magnetic, moderate corrosion resistance, lower cost. Examples: 430, 410. Used in automotive trim and kitchen equipment.
  • Martensitic (400 series / SUS 4xx): Magnetic, hardenable by heat treatment, good strength. Examples: 410, 420, 440C. Used in cutlery, bearings, and surgical instruments.
  • Duplex: Combined austenitic and ferritic structure, high strength and stress-corrosion resistance. Examples: SUS 329J3L (2205), SUS 329J4L.
  • Precipitation Hardening: High strength achieved by aging treatment. Examples: SUS 630 (17-4 PH), SUS 631 (17-7 PH).

Alloy and Carbon Steel

Carbon steels are classified by carbon content: low carbon (up to 0.3%), medium carbon (0.3 to 0.6%), and high carbon (above 0.6%). Alloy steels add elements such as chromium, molybdenum, nickel, and vanadium to enhance specific properties including hardenability, toughness, and wear resistance.

  • 4xxx series (AISI/SAE): Chromium-molybdenum steels (e.g. 4130, 4140, 4340). Common in aerospace and high-strength structural applications.
  • 5xxx series: Chromium steels (e.g. 5130, 5140). Used in gears, shafts, and fasteners.
  • ML prefix (Chinese): Cold heading quality steels used for fastener manufacturing.

Tool Steel

Tool steels are designed for cutting, forming, and die applications. They must maintain hardness at elevated temperatures and resist wear and deformation under heavy loads.

  • Carbon Tool Steel (SK series / W series): Lower cost, used for hand tools and low-speed cutting applications.
  • High-Speed Tool Steel (SKH series / M and T series): Retain hardness at cutting temperatures. M2 (SKH51) is the most common high-speed steel worldwide.
  • Alloy Tool Steel (SKS, SKD series / H, D, A series): Include hot work (H series), cold work (D, A series), and shock-resisting grades.

Structural Steel

Structural steels are identified primarily by their German W.Nr. (material number) in this tool, cross-referenced to DIN, GB, BS, EN, AFNOR, UNI, and other systems. They range from plain carbon grades (C15, C45) to chromium-molybdenum and nickel-chromium alloy grades used in pressure vessels, gears, and structural components.

Free Cutting Steel (SUM Series)

Free cutting steels contain additions of sulfur, lead, or both to improve machinability by promoting chip breakage. The JIS G 4804 SUM series covers 15 grades. While machining efficiency is excellent, weldability and ductility are reduced compared to standard grades. Leaded grades (SUM22L, SUM22L, SUM24L) are subject to restrictions in some regions due to environmental regulations.

Spring Steel (SUP Series)

Spring steels require high fatigue strength, high elastic limit, and good resistance to relaxation. Silicon, chromium, and vanadium are common alloying elements. The JIS G 4801 SUP series covers silicon-manganese, chromium, chromium-vanadium, and chromium-molybdenum grades.

Bearing Steel (SUJ Series)

Bearing steels require high hardness, wear resistance, and fatigue strength under cyclic contact loading. SUJ2 (equivalent to 52100 / 100Cr6) is the most widely used bearing steel globally and serves as the benchmark for this category.

Heat-Resisting Steel (SUH Series)

Heat-resisting steels maintain their mechanical properties and oxidation resistance at elevated temperatures (above 500 degrees C). The JIS G 4311 SUH series includes both austenitic and ferritic grades, used in exhaust valves, furnace components, and high-temperature fasteners.

Equivalency Lookup Best Practices

How to Use This Tool Effectively

  • Search by grade designation: Enter the grade you know (e.g. 304L, 42CrMo4, SKH51) in the search box. The tool searches all standard fields simultaneously.
  • Filter by material type first: Narrowing by type (e.g. Stainless Steel, Tool Steel) reduces noise and returns more focused results, especially for common designations that appear across multiple categories.
  • Filter by standard: If you are looking for equivalents in a specific country standard, select that standard in the dropdown. Only records with a value in that standard field will appear.
  • Use the Compare feature: Select up to 5 cards using the circle button on each card, then click Compare to view a side-by-side table of all available standard designations. This is especially useful when evaluating multiple candidate substitutes.
  • Check source standards: Each card shows the JIS standard number (e.g. JIS G 4303) or source dataset. This tells you which standard family the primary designation belongs to.

Limitations to Be Aware Of

  • Approximate equivalency: Cross-reference tables represent closest matches, not identical grades. Chemical composition ranges may overlap but are not always identical.
  • Superseded standards: Some designations (particularly older BS and national standards) have been withdrawn or replaced by EN equivalents. Always check the current status of a standard before specifying it.
  • Regional availability: A grade listed as equivalent may not be commercially available in all regions. Consult your SBS representative for availability guidance.
  • Application-specific requirements: Product standards (ASTM A193, A320, etc.) specify additional requirements beyond chemistry that equivalency tables do not address.

Standards Library: The full text of many standards referenced in this tool is available through the SBS Consensus Standards Library. Accessing the authoritative standard document is always recommended before making a final material selection or substitution decision.

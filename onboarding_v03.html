Executive Summary

The OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturer) remains responsible for final part approval because the OEM owns the application, assembly conditions, compliance, safety requirements, and shipped product. SBS supports that decision by defining part conformance, documenting assumptions, identifying risks, and recommending the appropriate approval path.

What SBS solves

Many OEMs have thousands of fastener and hardware part numbers with incomplete ERP descriptions, limited drawings, inconsistent samples, and mixed approval requirements. SBS creates technical structure from that messy data so implementation can proceed with full control and transparency.

What the process prevents

The process helps OEM engineering and quality teams avoid time-consuming approval work on low-value fasteners and hardware, while preventing avoidable disruption caused by part mismatches, undocumented assumptions, compliance issues, or unclear approval responsibility. Every step is visible to the OEM, so nothing proceeds without documented control.

Why fasteners need a different approval approach
Fasteners and hardware can represent up to 80% of BOM line items but only 3 to 5% of total spend. A one-size-fits-all approval process can overload engineering and quality teams with excessive review of low-value standard parts. SBS helps apply the right level of review where it matters most.
OEM vs. SBS responsibility
SBS defines part conformance and supports the OEM agreed approval process with samples, part categorization, attributes, documents, assumptions, deviations, test data, and recommendations. The OEM makes the final approval decision because only the OEM fully understands the end application, end customer expectations, and product-level risk.

Why Partner with SBS

The OEM is the expert in manufacturing its own product. SBS is the expert in part configuration, sourcing, procurement, quality, and logistics. Let SBS do the heavy lifting on fasteners and hardware so OEM engineering can stay focused on the core product.

OEM Expertise

OEMs know their product, application, and end customer better than anyone. That expertise belongs on the product, not on low-value fastener administration.

SBS Expertise

SBS has implemented VMI programs and part reviews with hundreds of OEMs across many different industries. The OEM can rely on SBS to share best practices and develop a bespoke solution that best meets their requirements, with full control and transparency at every step.

8-Step Process Flow

Hover over each node to see additional detail.

The process starts with OEM-provided part scope and ends with OEM approval and SBS production release. PIM means Part Information Management. COTS means Commercial Off-the-Shelf parts, typically standard parts or catalog parts.

1

Define Scope

OEM provides part list, known requirements, and available documentation.

Typical inputs
  • ERP, BOM, supplier list, or usage extract
  • OEM part number, description, EAU, location
  • Available prints and data sheet collection
  • Known quality, compliance, or criticality information
  • SBS may identify additional parts during site discovery
2

Collect Samples

SBS or OEM collects Current-Use Golden Samples.

Sample control
  • Ideally five new, unused pieces per part number
  • Collected from inventory, work cells, or stock rooms
  • Bagged by OEM part number and location
  • Retained by SBS and photographed under controlled conditions
3

Identify and Categorize

SBS classifies the part using PIM logic.

Attribute assignment
  • Category, type, standard, size, thread, geometry
  • Material, grade, hardness, finish, coating
  • Compliance needs such as RoHS, DFARS, CoO, non-China
  • Confirmed and inferred attributes are clearly noted
4

Review Assumptions

Open items are flagged before sourcing.

Conflict examples
  • Sample does not match OEM description or print
  • Multiple samples exist under the same part number
  • Current-use part does not meet stated standard
  • Compliance or application criticality is unclear
5

Source and Quote

SBS identifies standard, COTS, custom, or alternate paths.

Sourcing paths
  • Consensus-standard part
  • COTS catalog part
  • SBS-controlled standard part
  • Reverse-engineered special part
  • Alternate CoO or compliance-driven substitute
6

Submit Samples

SBS provides proposed sample and documentation.

Submission contents
  • SBS proposed sample
  • Comparison to Current-Use Golden Sample
  • Assumptions and deviations
  • Compliance documents, when required
  • Recommended approval path
7

Select Approval Path

SBS and OEM choose the right-sized method.

Approval options
  • Website Part Page
  • TDS or SBS Technical Print
  • FAI
  • PPAP Level 3 or Level 4
  • CPAL for high-value or critical parts
8

Approve and Release

OEM approves; SBS releases for production supply.

Release control
  • OEM records final approval
  • PSW approval when PPAP is required
  • SBS releases production quantities
  • Controlled documentation remains available for future use

Executive Summary

Risk in the onboarding and cost reduction process typically comes from incomplete information, mismatched samples, unclear compliance requirements, or approval effort that does not match part value. SBS identifies these risks early and recommends mitigation paths so the OEM can make the best informed decision.

What creates risk

Mismatched samples, undocumented assumptions, unclear compliance requirements, and approval effort that does not match part value are the most common sources of risk as parts move from the OEM to SBS.

How SBS manages it

SBS documents assumptions, flags conflicts in a shared master file, applies decision gates before formal validation work, and keeps the OEM as final decision-maker on anything with unresolved application or compliance risk.

Common conflict and risk triggers
  • Sample does not match OEM description or print
  • Multiple samples exist under the same part number
  • Current-use part does not meet the stated standard
  • Compliance or application criticality is unclear
How SBS handles missing or conflicting information
Missing information does not stop the project. SBS documents assumptions, identifies whether they are simple or sourcing-critical, and flags conflicts in a shared master file. Examples include samples that do not match prints, multiple samples under one part number, unclear compliance requirements, or current-use parts that do not meet the stated standard.
OEM vs. SBS responsibility for risk decisions

The OEM makes the final approval decision because only the OEM fully understands the end application, end customer expectations, and product-level risk.

Risk Review and Decision Gates

Risk Review Questions

  • Could a conforming alternate behave differently than the current-use part?
  • Are CoO, DFARS, RoHS, REACH, or end customer restrictions confirmed?
  • Does the part have known quality history or application criticality?
  • Will the approval path create more cost than the savings opportunity?

Decision Gates

  • Proceed: savings and risk profile justify implementation
  • Proceed with limits: use lower-effort approval path or phased rollout
  • Hold: missing application, compliance, or quality information
  • Stop: validation cost or risk exceeds savings value

Mitigation Strategies

  • Right-size the approval method to the part's actual risk and value
  • Log assumptions and conflicts in the shared master file as soon as they appear
  • Keep OEM sign-off on any part with unclear application, safety, or compliance impact
  • Apply decision gates before starting formal testing or validation work
  • Confirm CoO, DFARS, RoHS, and REACH status before sourcing compliance-sensitive parts

Executive Summary

Cost saving is a process, not a one-off event. SBS Advantage delivers sustainable cost savings through technical expertise and global sourcing, keeping OEM engineering engaged early and building a repeatable, disciplined cadence instead of a single piece-price exercise.

What undermines savings

Poorly defined objectives, a lack of focus beyond piece price, underfunded validation and testing, and lost momentum are the most common reasons cost saving initiatives fail to deliver.

The SBS approach

Maximize savings using the 80:20 rule, take a deep dive on the BOM to review and categorize every part, streamline the approval process, and hold regular engagement and governance meetings.

Why cost saving initiatives fail
  • Hard versus soft cost dollar savings are not distinguished, and supplier consolidation or part standardization goals are unclear
  • Every commodity or supplier is tackled at once instead of applying the 80:20 rule, and focus stays on piece price instead of total cost
  • Engineering is engaged late and treated only as a supply chain initiative
  • Tooling and engineering validation, including testing and PPAP, is not budgeted, and the engineering effort to validate is underestimated
  • Enthusiasm and engagement fade before savings are realized, delaying results by a year or more
Discipline over time
Maintain engagement, accelerate validation, and realize savings. Cost saving is a process, not a one-off event, and SBS structures it as an ongoing, governed cadence rather than a single initiative. A senior champion should be identified on the OEM side to secure resource commitment and keep governance transparent and regular.

Executive Summary

Fasteners and hardware are high in part count but low in value, which makes them one of the best opportunities for standardization. SBS partners with OEM engineering, purchasing, and production teams to consolidate fastener types without compromising performance, and to keep new part creation under control going forward.

Why standardize

Standardization reduces procurement and inventory cost, simplifies sourcing, speeds up assembly, improves quality control, and makes maintenance and future design changes easier.

What SBS delivers

SBS runs a structured, three-step methodology, audit, standardize, and monitor, that consolidates fastener types and keeps the resulting catalog under control with full transparency for the OEM.

Why manufacturers should prioritize standardization
  • Cost savings: lower procurement, inventory, and tooling costs
  • Simplified supply chain: fewer suppliers and improved lead times
  • Enhanced efficiency: faster assembly and reduced complexity
  • Improved quality control: consistent, standards-based parts with fewer selection errors
  • Easier maintenance and repairs: replacement parts are readily available
  • Increased scalability: production scales without added tooling or inventory complexity
  • Environmental benefits: less waste from excess materials and packaging
  • Design flexibility: modular designs support future product adaptations

Why Standardize Fasteners

Cost and Inventory

  • Reduced procurement costs from bulk purchasing of standardized fasteners
  • Lower inventory, storage, handling, and administrative costs
  • Minimized tooling, tapping, drilling, and punching costs

Supply Chain and Efficiency

  • Streamlined sourcing with fewer suppliers and stronger relationships
  • Improved lead times versus custom or rare parts
  • Faster assembly and fewer errors with fewer fastener types to handle

Quality and Maintenance

  • Consistent quality and reliability against ANSI or ISO standards
  • Fewer errors selecting or installing the wrong part
  • Easier repairs since correct replacement parts are readily available

Scalability and Sustainability

  • Production scales without added tooling or inventory adjustments
  • Less waste from excess materials and packaging
  • Modular, interchangeable designs support future product updates

3-Step Standardization Approach

SBS supports the OEM through all three steps, from cataloging existing fasteners to enforcing approval controls once standards are in place.

Step 1: Audit and Analyze

  • Catalog existing fasteners by type, specification, material, and application
  • Identify redundancies and consolidate overlapping fastener types
  • Assess whether custom or specialized fasteners can be replaced with standard parts
  • Involve design, engineering, production, and procurement teams

Step 2: Develop and Implement

  • Set design standards aligned to ANSI or ISO for common use cases
  • Eliminate non-standard or rarely used fasteners
  • Require Engineering Manager or Purchasing approval before any new fastener part number is created
  • Update CAD libraries and blueprints with standardized options
  • Leverage supplier relationships for bulk sourcing

Step 3: Monitor and Enforce

  • Track fastener usage across the organization
  • Regularly evaluate whether standardized parts still meet requirements
  • Train design, production, and procurement staff
  • Continuously improve the program as materials and standards evolve

Final Key Takeaways

SBS performs the technical heavy lifting so OEM engineering and quality teams can focus approval effort where it matters most: high-risk, high-value, complex, or compliance-sensitive parts.

  • A robust, collaborative, and transparent implementation plan mitigates the risk of production turbulence.
  • Strategic cost reduction delivers sustainable savings only when it is run as an ongoing, disciplined process, not a one-time event.
  • Part standardization and consolidation reduce cost, complexity, and risk while improving quality and scalability.

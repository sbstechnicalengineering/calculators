Understanding Hydrogen Embrittlement in Fasteners

Hydrogen embrittlement (HE) causes unexpected brittle fracture in high-strength steels without visible warning. It is driven by three overlapping conditions: tensile stress, a hydrogen source, and a susceptible material. This guide explains IHE and EHE, how to identify risk, and how to eliminate it. Key references: ASTM F1941 and IFI-142.

Types of Hydrogen Embrittlement
IHE: Internal Hydrogen Embrittlement

Internal Hydrogen Embrittlement

IHE occurs when atomic hydrogen is absorbed into the metal before or during manufacturing, then trapped by a dense coating layer.

  • Acid pickling and descaling: HCl or H2SO4 reacts with steel; atomic hydrogen diffuses into the part
  • Electroplating: both the acid clean and the plating bath introduce hydrogen
  • Welding: moisture or flux dissociation introduces hydrogen in the heat-affected zone
  • Steel melting: hydrogen can be trapped during solidification at the mill

Under tensile stress hydrogen migrates to stress concentration points (first engaged thread, underhead fillet) and initiates cracking. Failure can be sudden and without visible deformation or warning.

EHE: Environmental Hydrogen Embrittlement

Environmental Hydrogen Embrittlement

EHE occurs when hydrogen is introduced after installation from the service environment. Also called Stress Corrosion Cracking (SCC) when corrosion drives the mechanism.

  • Galvanic coupling: fastener acting as cathode; cathodic reaction generates H+
  • General corrosion: moisture and steel react; hydrogen released at the surface
  • Sour or H2S service: sulfide ions inhibit recombination, forcing atomic H into the lattice
  • High-pressure steam: 4Fe plus 6H2O produces significant H2
  • Caustic solutions: accelerated hydrogen uptake at elevated pH

EHE is time-dependent. Parts may survive installation but fail weeks or months later. It is often the least understood and most dangerous corrosion-related failure mode.

Elimination and Mitigation
IHE Control: Baking

Post-Plate Baking for Hydrogen Relief

Baking after electroplating drives trapped hydrogen out by thermal diffusion. Per ASTM F1941 and IFI-142:

01

Complete electroplating

Finish plating per specification.

02

Bake within 4 hours (preferably 1 hour) of plating

Delay allows hydrogen to migrate to stress zones before removal (ASTM F1941 Section 6.4.2.2).

03

Bake 375 to 450 deg F (190 to 232 deg C) for 2 to 24 hours

Typical: 8 hours at 400 deg F. Higher hardness requires longer baking. Duration agreed between purchaser and manufacturer.

04

Bake BEFORE applying chromate passivate

Temperatures above 150 deg F damage chromate film, negating corrosion protection (ASTM F1941 Section 6.4.2.2).

05

Verify via ASTM F1940 process control test

Sustained-load test confirms process does not introduce embrittlement for the product class.

Note: Even correct baking does not guarantee complete hydrogen removal. For HRC above 40, electroplating should be avoided where possible. Specify Geomet 321 or equivalent dip-spin finish instead.
IHE Prevention: Alternate Finishes

Outgassing-Friendly Alternate Finishes

These finishes use abrasive blast (not acid) and a porous zinc-flake layer that does not introduce or trap hydrogen:

  • Geomet 321: inorganic zinc flake; no acid; porous layer permits outgassing; 720 plus hour salt spray; RoHS and REACH compliant; no hexavalent chromium. Preferred recommendation for high-strength fasteners.
  • Geomet 500 or Dacromet: higher build; zinc-aluminum flake; suitable for Grade 10.9 and 12.9 metric and Grade 8 and 9 SAE.
  • Delta Protekt KL100 or VH300: dip-spin zinc flake; no acid; suitable for HRC above 40.
  • Mechanical Zinc (ASTM B695): impact plating; no acid clean; porous coating allows natural outgassing.
  • Sherardizing: thermal diffusion zinc; no liquid acid; suitable for high-strength parts including springs and pins.
Geomet 321 is the preferred drop-in alternative to zinc electroplate for high-strength fasteners where IHE risk exists and corrosion performance requirements can be met.
EHE Control

Reducing Environmental HE and Stress Corrosion

  • Reduce hardness: below HRC 35 where design permits. Below HRC 22 there is negligible susceptibility for most environments.
  • Avoid cathodic coupling: do not use steel fasteners as cathode in a dissimilar-metal assembly without insulating washers or sleeves.
  • Barrier coatings: Geomet, zinc flake, or HDG reduce corrosion-driven hydrogen generation at the surface.
  • Remove stress raisers: generous fillet radii, smooth thread runout, no sharp notches.
  • Sour service (H2S): consult a materials engineer; hardness limits are much stricter than standard grades for H2S environments.
  • Cathodic protection: impressed-current CP systems can drive hydrogen into connected fasteners if current density is not managed.
